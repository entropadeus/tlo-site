The Biggest Mistake Texas Parents Make with Their Wills

November 30, 2024 4 min read

You've named guardians for your kids, but have you made this critical error? One oversight could leave your children's inheritance tied up in court for months.

You did the responsible thing. You created a will, named guardians for your children, and felt that sense of relief knowing you'd protected your family. But if you're like 70% of Texas parents, you've made a critical mistake that could cost your children thousands of dollars and months of court proceedings.

The Mistake: Leaving Assets Directly to Minor Children

Here's what most parents don't realize: In Texas, minor children cannot legally inherit assets directly. If you leave everything to your kids without proper planning, here's what actually happens:

  • The court must appoint a guardian of the estate (even if you named a guardian for their care)
  • Annual accountings and bonds are required
  • Every significant expense needs court approval
  • Legal fees can consume 3-5% of the estate annually
  • When your child turns 18, they receive everything outright—ready or not

Real-Life Scenario

Sarah and Mike had a simple will leaving everything to each other, then to their three children equally. When both died in a car accident, their $500,000 estate should have provided security for their kids. Instead:

  • The guardian had to petition the court to access funds for the children's needs
  • Private school tuition required court approval each semester
  • Medical expenses beyond routine care needed documentation
  • The eldest received $166,666 on his 18th birthday—and spent it within two years

The Solution: Testamentary Trusts

A testamentary trust in your will can prevent all of this. Here's how it works:

You Control the Terms:

  • Choose who manages the money (your trusted brother, not the court)
  • Set distribution ages (maybe 25, 30, and 35 instead of 18)
  • Define acceptable uses (education, health, starting a business)
  • Include protection from creditors and divorcing spouses

Flexibility When Needed:

  • Allow distributions for emergencies
  • Permit early access for education or home purchases
  • Give trustees discretion to handle unexpected situations

Common Concerns (and Why They're Wrong)

"Trusts are only for rich people"
If you own a home and have life insurance, you have enough to justify a trust. The cost of not having one far exceeds the cost of setting one up.

"I trust my kids to be responsible"
It's not about trust—it's about protection. An 18-year-old with a large inheritance faces pressures you can't imagine: "friends" with business ideas, predatory lenders, and their own inexperience.

"It's too complicated"
A testamentary trust adds just a few pages to your will but provides decades of protection. Your attorney handles the complexity; you just make the big decisions.

"The question isn't whether you trust your children. It's whether you want to protect them from making life-altering financial mistakes before they're truly ready."

Beyond the Basics: Other Critical Provisions

While you're fixing this issue, consider these often-missed provisions:

  • Digital asset access: Include language allowing guardians to access online accounts
  • Pet care: Yes, you need to plan for Fluffy too
  • Personal property memorandum: Avoid family fights over sentimental items
  • Guardian compensation: Consider allowing reasonable payment for the person raising your children

The 18-Year-Old Lottery Winner Problem

Statistics show that lottery winners under 25 are broke within five years 70% of the time. Why would an inheritance be different? By structuring distributions over time, you give your children the gift of maturity and financial education before they receive large sums.

Take Action Today

If your will leaves assets directly to minor children, you're not alone—but you are at risk. The fix is straightforward:

  1. Review your current will
  2. Decide on trust terms that reflect your values
  3. Update your documents
  4. Sleep better knowing you've truly protected your children

Your children deserve more than money—they deserve the wisdom and protection that comes with proper planning. Don't let a preventable mistake turn your legacy into a legal nightmare.
