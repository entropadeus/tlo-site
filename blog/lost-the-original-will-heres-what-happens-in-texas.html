Lost the Original Will? Here's What Happens in Texas

October 15, 2024 6 min read

When dad's will is nowhere to be found, Texas law has specific procedures. Understanding your options can save time, money, and family conflict during an already difficult time.

The house has been searched top to bottom. Every filing cabinet, safe deposit box, and desk drawer has been emptied. Dad always said he had a will, multiple family members saw him sign it, but the original is nowhere to be found. Now what?

First, Don't Panic: Copies Can Work

Texas law allows for probating a copy of a will, but it requires jumping through additional hoops. The court starts with a presumption: if the original will can't be found and was last in the testator's possession, it's assumed they destroyed it with the intent to revoke it.

Overcoming the Presumption

To probate a copy of a lost will in Texas, you must prove by clear and convincing evidence that:

  • The will was not revoked by the decedent
  • The will was executed in compliance with Texas law
  • The copy accurately reflects the original will's contents

Evidence That Helps Your Case

  • Witness testimony: People who saw the original will or heard the decedent discuss it
  • Attorney records: Copies in the drafting attorney's files
  • Multiple copies: Identical copies found in different locations
  • Digital records: Email exchanges about the will's contents
  • Family discussions: Evidence the decedent intended the will to remain valid

When the Will Can't Be Probated

If you can't meet the burden of proof, the estate will be distributed according to:

  • Any previous valid will, or
  • Texas intestacy laws if no prior will exists
"The best defense against a lost will is proper storage and communication. Keep originals in a fireproof safe or attorney's office, and make sure your family knows where to find them."

Prevention is Better Than Litigation

To avoid this situation:

  • Store the original will in a safe, accessible location
  • Tell trusted family members where to find it
  • Keep copies in multiple locations
  • Consider storing the original with your attorney
  • Document any will updates or revisions

Losing a will creates stress during an already difficult time. While Texas law provides options for probating copies, the process is more complex and expensive than probating an original. The best approach is prevention through proper storage and communication with your family.
